Abstract

The current focus of AI research is shifting from emphasizing model training towards enhancing evaluation quality, a transition that is crucial for driving further advancements in AI systems. Traditional evaluation methods typically rely on reward models assigning scalar preference scores to outputs. Although effective, such approaches lack interpretability, leaving users often uncertain about why a reward model rates a particular response as high or low. The advent of LLM-as-a-Judge provides a more scalable and interpretable method of supervision, offering insights into the decision-making process. Moreover, with the emergence of large reasoning models, which consume more tokens for deeper thinking and answer refinement, scaling test-time computation in the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm presents an avenue for further boosting performance and providing more interpretability through reasoning traces. In this paper, we introduce J1-7B, which is first supervised fine-tuned on reflection-enhanced datasets collected via rejection-sampling and subsequently trained using Reinforcement Learning (RL) with verifiable rewards. At inference time, we apply Simple Test-Time Scaling (STTS) strategies for additional performance improvement. Experimental results demonstrate that J1-7B surpasses the previous state-of-the-art LLM-as-a-Judge by 4.8% and exhibits a 5.1% stronger scaling trend under STTS. Additionally, we present three key findings: (1) Existing LLM-as-a-Judge does not inherently exhibit such scaling trend. (2) Model simply fine-tuned on reflection-enhanced datasets continues to demonstrate similarly weak scaling behavior. (3) Significant scaling trend emerges primarily during the RL phase, suggesting that effective STTS capability is acquired predominantly through RL training. We hope that our comprehensive analysis will provide insights to the community and contribute to the broader AI alignment community, especially in the context of developing more robust and scalable evaluation systems.

Why STTS for LLM-as-a-Judge?

The evolving landscape of model evaluation with three approaches: the Bradley-Terry Model, LLM-as-a-Judge with Parallel Decoding, and LLM-as-a-Judge enhanced with Simple Test-Time Scaling (STTS). Traditional models like Bradley-Terry offer a basic scalar approach but fall short in interpretability and scalability. In contrast, LLM-as-a-Judge, by leveraging the power of large language models, provides a more scalable and interpretable framework for evaluation.

Introduction

Our work focuses on enhancing the reasoning ability of LLM-as-a-Judge, aiming to improve its decision-making process and the quality of its evaluations. To achieve this, we propose a novel method that integrates Simple Test-Time Scaling (STTS), which encourages the model to reflect on its initial judgments and refine its decisions.

The method begins with Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to train the model on a carefully curated dataset. Specifically, we employ a rejection sampling technique to collect reasoning trajectories, ensuring that only those responses that initially seem incorrect, but are corrected through reflection, are retained. This iterative process helps the model to consider more detailed aspects of the responses, strengthening its decision-making capability.

To further optimize performance, we introduce a Reinforcement Learning (RL) phase, where the model learns from feedback using a reward-based approach. The reward structure is designed to reinforce correct judgments, with a focus on improving interpretability and accuracy. Additionally, we incorporate a budget-forcing mechanism to elicit the reflective reasoning ability learned from above training process.

Through this approach, we demonstrate how our method empowers LLM-as-a-Judge to make more informed, accurate, and interpretable decisions, enhancing its overall reasoning capacity.

To encompass current model ability to do STTS, we propose a two-stage training pipeline. Firstly, we utilize reflection-enhanced rejection sampling to supervised fine-tuning a base model. Secondly, we use RL training to further elicit model's reasoning ability. After such pipeline, J1-7B demonstrate boosted performance and better scaling trend.

Key Contributions

  • We identify that existing LLM-as-a-Judge models trained solely on general judgment datasets exhibit limited scaling behavior under STTS, highlighting an important yet underexplored limitation in current frameworks.
  • We introduce a novel two-stage training paradigm, starting with a reflection-enhanced SFT dataset curated through rejection sampling, effectively initializing the LLM-as-a-Judge to optimally leverage reflective reasoning tokens introduced by STTS.
  • We demonstrate that further enhancements in scaling behavior emerge explicitly during the RL stage, indicating that the capability for effective reflective reasoning under STTS is actively learned and reinforced through RL process.
  • Empirically, we validate our proposed method by introducing J1-7B that surpasses previous state-of-the-art baselines. Specifically, J1-7B achieves a 4.8% improvement in overall judgment performance and exhibits a 5.1% stronger scaling trend under STTS, confirming the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed training methodology.

RQ 3What factors influence the performance of J1-7B during STTS?

3.1 Impact of Cold-start Dataset Composition

Analysis of how different compositions of the cold-start dataset affect model performance. We show that incorporating reasoning-intensive datasets into the cold-start dataset can both improve model performance and STTS scaling trend.

3.2 Choice of RL Algorithm

Comparison of different RL algorithms and their impact on model performance. Typically, we find that GRPO shows inferior STTS scaling trend in overall. It might due to the response length of GRPO is significantly longer than other methods.

We also shows that GRPO's original reflection frequency is higher than other methods, thus further STTS that excessively increases the use of reflection words may negatively impact overall performance.

3.3 Training Stage Progression

Analysis of how model performance evolves through training progress. The results show that the most significant improvements in STTS capability emerge during the RL phase.
